Chapter Eighteen

Marks of those who return from heaven

Devī spoke:

|18.1| Please tell me now about the signs of those who, after having experienced enjoyable things at pleasure for a long time, thus their merits having worn away, return to the mortal world, and especially about the fruits of their deeds, one by one.

Eight kinds of donation

Maheśvara spoke:

|18.2| He who always gives to the poor and to the ones afflicted by pain will experience enjoyments in Īśaloka together with Apsarases for millions of years, before he returns to the world of mortals, his merits having worn away.

|18.3| [These] men will be born [again] in divine families, [later] having a wife and wealth and many servants, into families that are thick with wealth consists of cows, horses, jewels etc., he himself possessing shining beauty and loveliness.

|18.4| [If one] donates clothes to a Brahmin respectfully, he will have fun in the heavens for millions of years. When they return to the world of mortals, their characteristics mark is that they rise to an extremely glorious rank.

|18.5| He who donates wells, fountains, or lotus-ponds will reach the world of Jaleśvara [i.e. Varuṇa]. Then falling from that world, he will be [re-]born into a very comfortable family, and will be happy.

|18.6| By donating as little gold as a cubit, people can reach the world of Surendra [i.e. Indra]. The characteristic mark of those who fall from there to the world of mortals is prosperity, riches, wealth, and good fortune.

|18.7| By donating an excellent piece of land to a Brahmin without corruption[?], he will reach the world of Sureśvara. After experiencing enjoyments, he falls back to the world of mortals, And will possess the characteristic mark of sovereignty.

|18.8| He who donates sesame seeds to a Brahmin respectfully will reach the world of Keśava [i.e. Viṣṇu]. Then, having fallen and returned to the world of mortals, he will possess the characteristic mark of undiminishing acquisition of wealth.

|18.9| By donating beautiful cows to Brahmins, people reach Goloka. At the end of the æon, they return to the world of mortals. Their characteristic mark would be an abundance of cows with a hundred cows.

|18.10| The characteristic marks of those who have been in heaven are: an abundance of wealth, grace, the attainment of happiness and enjoyment, [a long] life, glory, beauty, family, sons, success, power, a glorious family, and riches.

Marks of those who return from hell

|18.11| I have taught you the eight supreme kinds of donation, the characteristic marks, and the [corresponding] worlds in brief. Listen, O Goddess, to the characteristic marks of those who have returned from hell, and to their actions and the fruiton thereof.

|18.12| If one hurts a Brahmin mentally of verbally, one goes to the boundaries of terrible hell. Gradually experiencing [his karmas] for eighty years in hell, he will live as an animal for millions [of years/lives].

|18.13| Those men will be [re-]born without any knowledge, will live on the fringes of town, and will lack family and wealth. They will always be tormented by uncurable diseases. These are the characteristic marks of one who endangeres the life of a Brahmin.

|18.14| If a Brahmin drinks alcohol, or out of curiosity smells [its] odour on his own accord, he will go to the terrible and unbearable hell for ten æons to experience [his karmas] there.

|18.15| Experiencing all the pain of animal existence, he will, with great difficulty, be [re-]born as a human. He will go through [states of being] a Caṇḍāla, a butcher, and a dog-cooker. In this case, he characteristic mark is that his palate becomes black.

|18.16| Those who despise the Vedas will [be reborn] with their tongues ... He who gives false testimony will [be reborn] blind[?]. [In case of] the murder of a friend, [one will experience] a hundred deaths in the womb. Those who eat forbidden food will [be reborn] as one who eats [only] leftovers.


Chapter Twenty-four

|24.1| ...

|24.61| This is the extent of Bhūrloka. Above it there is Bhuvaḥ, just beyond that Svarloka, and above that Maharloka,

|24.62| Janaloka, Tapoloka, and Satyaloka, in due order. Satya[loka] is said to be Brahmaloka and above it is located Viṣṇuloka.

|24.63| Beyond that, the great city of divine visions is to be recognized as a thousand-story palace with gates [decorated] with cat’s-eye gems and

|24.64| coloured with different kinds of precious stones, inhabited by different troops of beings. That place is full of charming riches of all desires.

|24.65| There, on a divine throne which is ornamented with all kinds of precious stones, the Lord Rudra is sitting, the one who wears his matted hair marked with the Moon,

|24.66| the one with three eyes, chief of the three worlds, holding a trident, the ruler of the thirty [gods], together with Devī, he the illustrious one, surrounded by the Gaṇas,

|24.67| with Skanda and Nandi standing in front of him, in a crowd of a hundred lakh of Gaṇas, embellished with many beautiful Rudra girls.

|24.68| There are seven sacred and divine rivers there that drive away all sins, with golden sandbanks, embellished with gems for rocks:

|24.69| Pāvanī, Vareṇyā, Varārhā, Varadā, Varā, Vareśā, and Varabhadrā. The waters of these auspicious rivers are crystal clear.

|24.70| There are gardens with numerous flowers, and trees with gems for flowers and fruits, with fences made of different kinds of precious stones. They are ten thousand (ayuta) yojanas in extent.

|24.71| People there rejoice delighting in non-violence and truthfulness, avoiding lust and anger, constantly practising yogic meditation.

|24.72| All the cow-mothers live there, practising observances. Goloka and Śivaloka are established as being one and the same.










Notes



|18.3| Note the change from plural to singular in pādas a and b, the slightly irregular plural nominative puṃsaḥ in pāda a, and that sastrī might have been meant as a separate word, in the sense of sastrīkaḥ (‘married’). I take gaur-aśva° in pāda c as an alternative form of go-aśva°. See NARADAP 1.71.77ab for a compound similar to the one here: gajāśvaratharatnaiś ca grāmakṣetradhanādibhiḥ I have emended the text in pāda d to restore the meter.

|18.4| Note that pāda a can be considered metrical only if dvi in dvijasya does not make the previous syllable heavy (muta cum liquida). Note the plural nominative °koṭyaḥ in pāda b for a more standard accusative °koṭīḥ (from koṭī).

|18.5| The phrase sutṛpteṣu kuleṣu (lit. ‘into satified families’) is slightly odd.

|18.6| I have chosen lakṣmyāḥ in pāda d against lakṣyāḥ as a lectio difficilior. It is supposed to stand for a plural nominative.

|18.9| It seems that gavāṃ is meant to be a singular accusative of go. Or is it a different animal? See 18.9d.

|18.10| Note the seemingly accusative forms °lābham and °kīrtim (for lābhaḥ and kīrtir).

|18.11| Pāda a was most probably intended to say dānāṣṭakam uttamaṃ kīrtitaṃ te. The slightly odd phrase śṛṇotu devī, instead of a vocative with śṛṇu, is metri causa.

|18.12| Note the stem form tirya in pāda d (metri causa), and that the phrase śatāyutānām is ambiguous. Perhaps śatāyutābdam (for śatāyutāny abdāni) or śatāyutāni janmāni was meant.

|18.13| In pāda a, I take mānuṣa as a stem form noun (metri causa), and I have emended °vidyā to °vidyāḥ to turn hīnavidyā it into a bahuvrīhi compound.

|18.14| I take dvija in pāda a as a stem form noun (for dvijaḥ), and I connect kāmato vā to pāda b only because of the vā, although what was really meant is probably kāmato ’kāmato vā (‘intentionally or unintentionally’). Strictly speaking, pāda c is unmetrical, the last syllable of narakam ending in a light syllable. Word ending syllables are often treated as heavy in this text. In pāda d atra probably stands for tatra (narake). It is not clear why atra seemed better for the redactors.

|18.15| The syntax of pāda a is obscure. Perhaps tiryaś (‘being an animal’, ‘in an animal form’) would fit better. In pāda b, a bahuvrīhi compound is required, thus my emendation of °janma to °janmā.

|18.16| I take veda as a stem form noun in pāda a. I suspect that pāda a may have contained a reference to upajihvā, a disease of the tongue. Understans garhāśanocchiṣṭabhujo in pāda d as garhitāśanā ucchiṣṭabhujo with double sandhi.

|18.17| Note kurvati for kurvanti metri causa. One could also emend °sattvaṃ.

|24.64| Note samṛddhāni as a plural instrumental of samṛddhi?.
